## Lesson learned

- I should have established this from the beginning: **a proper client-provider relationship means you get presented with options, you make informed decisions, and nothing moves forward without your sign-off.** Without this, the AI made all the decisions and I inherited a project I didn't shape.

- **Set rules and keep control.** It's important to give clear rules to the AI and always keep an eye on what it does. You can even create a checklist or workflow and ask the model to validate its own work against it. Without constraints, the AI tends to take the fastest path, not necessarily the best one.

- **Design Thinking was abandoned.** At the beginning, the AI had set up a plan based on Design Thinking, but in the end it didn't follow it. It skipped the empathy and ideation phases and went straight to building. Lesson: if you choose a framework, you need to insist it's respected — otherwise it's just a decorative document.

- **Defining roles is essential.** I had to clarify that it's a software company and I'm the client/PO. Without this distinction, the AI acts as both the commissioner and the executor, and you become a spectator of your own project.

- **You don't need to know the language, you need to understand the process.** I don't know TypeScript or the Web Audio API, but I know exactly what each sound generator does procedurally. This allowed me to give precise feedback and optimize sessions — for example, during rapid sound feedback cycles, I asked to skip tests and checks to speed up iterations. Knowing *what* happens under the hood, even without knowing *how* it's written, is a huge advantage.

- **The product is good, the visuals are the weak point.** The sounds are convincing, the architecture is solid, but improving the visual side is hard with vibecoding. The AI can write CSS all day, but design requires a human eye and aesthetic sensibility that you don't get from "make it prettier." You'd need visual references, mockups, or a design system to follow.
